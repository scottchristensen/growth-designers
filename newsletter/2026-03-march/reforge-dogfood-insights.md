# Reforge Synthetic User Testing — Dogfood Results on GrowthDesigners.co

**Run #1**
- 12 sessions
- 1 persona: New growth designer - First time visitor
- 4 tasks
- 3 devices (Desktop, Default, Mobile)
- Completed in 3 min

**Impact Breakdown:** 18 high / 32 medium / 10 low
**Total Insights:** 60

---

## Executive Summary

We evaluated the first-time experience for a new growth designer across desktop, default, and mobile, focusing on the homepage's clarity about growth design and access to Tools, Growth Design School, Slack community, and the newsletter. The dominant pattern: the site asks users to join/subscribe/register before explaining what **growth design** is or how offerings fit a beginner's path. The most critical finding is the absence of a clear, above-the-fold definition of growth design, which undermines comprehension and confidence to engage further. Related frictions include a gated Slack join flow, a newsletter subscribe form with little context, and a School page that lacks pricing and beginner guidance.

---

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Define growth design upfront
Add a one-sentence, jargon-free definition of **growth design** in the hero and a visible "New here? Start here" path before join/subscribe CTAs to orient beginners. (3 insights)

### 2. Reduce Slack community friction and make it prominent
Replace "Apply Today" with a **Join our Slack** CTA placed higher on the page (especially mobile) and offer instant join or a lighter application with clear rationale and timeline. (3 insights)

### 3. Give newsletter context and previews before asking for email
Add a brief value prop and frequency near the subscribe form and link to **Recent Issues/Archive** so newcomers can sample content before subscribing. (3 insights)

### 4. Make the School transparent for beginners
Move curriculum and "Who it's for" above the fold, show **pricing** clearly, and provide a free preview lesson before the "Register now" ask. (3 insights)

### 5. Reframe Tools as a beginner-friendly toolkit
Balance partner links with education: categorize by task/level and explain each tool's role in growth design with starter guides and templates. (2 insights)

### 6. Fix the mobile menu overlay
When the hamburger menu opens, use an opaque background for legibility; desktop nav is already clear. (2 insights)

## Top Themes

### Explain It Before You Sell It: Define "Growth Design" Upfront
(5 insights)

Across viewports, the hero urges users to join and upskill without first defining growth design, leaving newcomers unsure what the site is about. Mobile and default views show prominent community CTAs, but no concise definition; on desktop, the best explanation hides in the footer. This creates early ambiguity that depresses engagement with School, Tools, Community, and Newsletter. Add a one-sentence definition in or directly below the hero and link a visible "New here? Start here" path that orients users to core concepts and free primers. Reinforce the definition across School and Tools intros and alongside newsletter previews to keep beginners grounded.

**Supporting insights:**
- The homepage hero section on mobile invites users to 'Join our community' and 'Upskill on growth design' but doesn't define what 'growth design' is above the fold. As a newcomer, I need a clear, concise definition upfront to understand the site's purpose.
- The hero section immediately asks to 'Join our community of Growth Designers' but doesn't explain what 'growth design' is. The subheadline mentions upskilling but lacks a clear definition or value proposition for a beginner.
- After scrolling down once, the page shows company logos and another headline 'Accelerate your impact', but still no definition of 'growth design'.
- The page strongly encourages joining the community and newsletter, and mentions courses and job insights, but still hasn't provided a clear definition of 'growth design' for a beginner.
- The clearest definition of GrowthDesigners ('a community for designers focused on blending growth strategies and design to make a lasting business impact') is hidden in the footer.

### Community Join ≠ College Application: Slack Access Creates Friction
(5 insights)

While desktop surfaces a clear community CTA, the join flow routes to an "Apply Today" Zapier form that feels exclusionary and slow. On mobile, the Slack CTA is hard to spot—often buried below newsletter and logos or embedded as indistinct text—so users hunting for community may miss it. This combination signals gatekeeping and delays, undermining momentum for first-time learners. Elevate a dedicated Join our Slack button (with Slack logo) within 1-2 scrolls on mobile and above the fold on desktop. Prefer instant Slack OAuth or a lightweight form; if curation remains, explain why, expected wait time, and explicitly welcome beginners with clear benefits.

**Supporting insights:**
- The 'Apply Today' button leads to a Zapier form requiring personal details and a role description to 'apply' for the Slack community.
- Even after scrolling further, the page talks about 'community learning' but lacks a clear, standalone 'Join our Slack' button. The focus seems diffused across courses, jobs, and community without a primary CTA for Slack.
- Found the 'Join our community' button, but it was located very far down the mobile page, requiring multiple scrolls past newsletter signups and other content.
- The 'Join our community' button led to a page specifically for the Slack community, which is good. However, the CTA is 'Apply Today', implying an application process rather than a direct/instant join.
- On the homepage, the CTA to join the community is very clear and visible above the fold. There's a 'Slack Community' link in the top navigation and a 'Join Community' button, both easy to spot within seconds.

### Don't Ask for Emails Blind: Newsletter Needs Context and Proof
(5 insights)

Users frequently encounter a subscribe box with no description, frequency, or previews. This feels premature and lowers conversion confidence. Although an archive exists and in-article CTAs are strong, it's not linked prominently from the homepage, and cross-promoted content risks confusing readers about what they're subscribing to. Add a short value prop and frequency label beside the subscribe form and surface a "Recent Issues" section with 1-3 snippets on the homepage. On the archive, provide clear navigation back to the main site and visually distinguish any cross-promotions. Keep prominent community CTAs at article ends to deepen engagement after content sampling.

**Supporting insights:**
- The 'Subscribe to our newsletter' section appears with just an email field and button, but no description of the newsletter content, frequency, or links to recent/past issues.
- On the homepage, there's a 'Subscribe to our newsletter' form visible without scrolling, but it lacks context. As a newbie, I want to know what the newsletter is about, how often it's sent, and see examples before subscribing. Just an email field feels premature.
- The homepage body does not contain any links to recent newsletter issues or an archive, only a direct subscribe call-to-action without context.
- On the newsletter archive page, past issues are freely accessible and recent, which is good for previewing. However, the topics, while relevant to growth, might assume some prior knowledge and don't explicitly define 'growth design' for a beginner. There's also no clear link back to the main community/website from this beehiiv subdomain.
- Within the article page for 'Growth Designers Monthly', there's a large section promoting a '2026 State of Marketing Report' requiring 'SUBSCRIBE AND UNLOCK'. This appears to be for a different newsletter/brand ('Marketing Against The Grain').

### School Calls to Action Outpace Clarity
(6 insights)

The School page pushes "Register now" and "masterclass" language early, yet omits essentials that beginners need: a clear Foundations path, curriculum depth, instructor credibility, free previews, and—critically—pricing. Key reassurance content (e.g., "Introduction & Foundations") is buried, and pricing is absent even near the bottom, stalling evaluation. Bring "Who it's for," a curriculum overview highlighting beginner modules, and transparent pricing above the fold. Add instructor bios and a sample lesson to de-risk the decision. Soften the CTA placement and wording until after details appear, and consider offering a stand-alone Foundations track or trial to seed trust.

**Supporting insights:**
- On the School page, the 'Register now' CTA appears very early, before details like curriculum, pricing, instructors, or free content previews are shown. The cartoon faces next to it don't add immediate clarity or trust.
- The School page, even after scrolling to near the bottom, does NOT display any pricing for the course. This is a major blocker for evaluation.
- Expanding the 'Product designers new to growth' section only gave a description, no pricing or preview link. The term 'masterclass' for a course also aimed at beginners is confusing.
- On the 'Growth Design School' landing page, the initial heading is clear, but the sub-heading is quite broad. For a beginner, it doesn't immediately tell me if there are entry-level courses or what the learning path looks like.
- Found the 'Curriculum Overview' after scrolling, with an 'Introduction & Foundations' module that looks promising for beginners. However, it took a lot of scrolling to find this, and pricing is still not visible.

### Tools Page Reads Like Affiliates, Not a Beginner's Toolkit
(4 insights)

The Tools section skews toward partner promotions with "Get" buttons and discounts, but offers little context on when or why a novice should use each tool. This can feel sales-forward and erode trust for learners seeking guidance. Reframe the page as a curated toolkit: categorize by task (analytics, experimentation, research), level (beginner/advanced), and journey stage. For each tool, add what it's for in growth design, when to use it, pros/cons, and links to tutorials or case studies. Visually distinguish partner promos from educational content, and include free, native templates or starter kits to deliver immediate value.

**Supporting insights:**
- The 'Tools' page currently looks like a list of partner promotions rather than a curated educational resource on tools for growth design. Each tool has a 'Get' button and discount, but lacks context on *why* a beginner growth designer should consider it or how it fits into their learning journey.
- The 'Tools' page appears to list recommended external/partner tools rather than free, internal resources or templates from growthdesigners.co, which is what I was hoping for as a beginner.
- On the 'Top Tools' page, the intro text is clear, but as a beginner, I'm not just looking for a list of tools, but also context on *why* certain tools are good for beginners or for specific growth design tasks.
- The 'Tools' page lists external tools, and 'Scrapbook' looks like a useful database of tactics for learning, which is good for a beginner. However, these are external, not part of the site's own school.
